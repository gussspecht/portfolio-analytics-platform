PortfolioIQ Education Center

Use this as a plain-English reference while reading your dashboard. The goal is to make every ratio actionable without hiding the math from users who want to go deeper.

Annualized Return

The average yearly growth rate implied by historical returns. It is different from cumulative total return: a portfolio can show +137% total over several years while the annualized rate is much lower.

Volatility

A measure of how much returns swing around their average. Higher volatility usually means a bumpier ride and a wider range of possible outcomes.

Sharpe Ratio

Return earned above the risk-free rate for each unit of total volatility. Around 1 is strong, 2 is exceptional, and below 1 deserves more scrutiny.

Sortino Ratio

Similar to Sharpe, but it only penalizes downside volatility. It is often more intuitive because upside surprises are not treated as bad risk.

Max Drawdown

The worst historical fall from a previous high to a later low. It answers: how painful could the ride have been before recovery?

Beta

How sensitive an asset or portfolio is to a benchmark such as the S&P 500. A beta above 1 tends to move more than the market; below 1 tends to move less.

Correlation

How similarly two assets move. Low or negative correlation can improve diversification because holdings do not all rise and fall together.

Value at Risk

An estimate of potential loss at a chosen confidence level. For example, 5% VaR approximates a bad-case loss threshold in the worst 5% of simulations.

Monte Carlo Simulation

A projection method that runs many random return paths to show a range of possible portfolio outcomes instead of one single forecast.

Allocation Weight

The percentage of your portfolio assigned to each holding. Weights drive portfolio return, risk, and concentration more than the number of tickers alone.

Diversification

The practice of spreading exposure across assets, sectors, and return drivers. Good diversification reduces dependence on a single holding or theme.

Risk-Free Rate

The return assumption for a very low-risk alternative, often based on Treasury yields. It is used as the baseline in Sharpe and Sortino calculations.

Portfolio Health Score

A composite score that blends return quality, volatility, drawdown, diversification, concentration, and benchmark sensitivity. It is a quick diagnosis, not a final investment decision.

Benchmark

A reference investment, such as SPY or QQQ, used to judge whether the portfolio is being rewarded for the risks it takes.

Alpha

The portfolio return above or below what its beta and benchmark return would imply. Positive alpha means the portfolio beat its risk-adjusted benchmark expectation historically.

Tracking Difference

The simple return gap between your portfolio and a benchmark over the same period. It shows whether you outperformed or lagged before adjusting for beta.

Rebalancing

Adjusting holdings back toward target weights after performance causes them to drift. It can reduce concentration and keep risk aligned with your plan.

Watchlist

A list of stocks or ETFs you want to monitor before buying. It keeps research candidates separate from actual portfolio holdings.

Dividend Yield

Annual dividends as a percentage of price. It estimates income yield, though dividends can change and high yield may signal business stress.

Portfolio Income

The estimated annual cash income from dividends based on your holdings, weights, investment value, and each holding's dividend yield.

Goal Planner

A planning view that compares your current portfolio return against the return needed to reach a target value after contributions.

Required Return

The annual growth rate needed to reach a target amount from your current value and planned contributions. If it is far above realistic market returns, the plan may need more time, savings, or lower risk expectations.

Scenario Preset

A projection setting that changes return, volatility, and shock assumptions. Conservative, base, and optimistic modes help show a range instead of pretending one forecast is certain.

Historical Bootstrap

A simulation method that randomly samples actual past daily portfolio returns. It keeps real historical patterns better than a purely normal distribution, though the future can still differ.

Fat-Tail Shock

A rare but severe simulated move that represents crashes happening more often than a normal bell curve would predict.

Stress Test

A deliberate adverse scenario, such as recession, inflation, rate spike, or company shock, used to test whether the portfolio can survive uncomfortable conditions.

Real Return

Investment return after subtracting inflation. It matters because a portfolio can grow in dollars while still losing purchasing power.

Inflation NPV

The inflation-adjusted value of projected wealth minus the present value of invested capital. It estimates wealth created beyond inflation and savings.

Export Report

A downloadable plain-text summary of portfolio metrics, risks, and recommendations. It is useful for review, presentations, or resume demos.
